XR Motion Capture Pipeline

During the summer, I developed an XR motion capture pipeline at Carnegie Mellon University to support research on interaction techniques in extended reality. I began by studying common XR interactions and identified selection and manipulation as the most widely used. To test the capabilities of the Meta SDK, I built a small prototype where a keypad would randomly spawn within the user’s field of view and could be selected using a poking gesture. This exploration informed the direction of the larger project, which aimed to provide developers with a flexible pipeline to prototype and test new XR menu configurations.


A core part of the system involved integrating the OptiTrack motion capture system into Unity. This required setting up the OptiTrack plugin, configuring the Motive software, and working with a motion capture suit. Because OptiTrack and Unity use different coordinate systems, I implemented a three-point calibration method that relied on 4x4 transformation matrices to align them accurately. This ensured that motion data collected from OptiTrack could be meaningfully combined with Unity’s XR environment.



Once the calibration system was in place, I designed and implemented a record and replay framework. The system logged transformation data from multiple sources — including OptiTrack skeletons, Meta Quest VR hands, and interactive objects like the keypad — into CSV files. To parse and reconstruct this data for replay, I used regular expressions, ensuring that each recorded session could be faithfully reproduced inside Unity. This made it possible for researchers to record motion capture sessions, play them back, and analyze user interactions with XR menus.



In the fall semester, I extended the pipeline into a VR exercise training prototype. This system allowed users to select an exercise, observe a recorded avatar demonstrating the movement, and rotate the character for a clearer view of any joint being exercised. We presented this prototype to ASICS researchers, highlighting its potential applications for sports training and interactive fitness.
